01 — The Engineering Problem

Why does model choice matter so much?

Choosing the wrong architecture for a physical system isn't just an academic mistake — it costs real compute time, real money, and produces models that hallucinate physics they were never taught.

5–10× Estimated extra compute when wrong architecture is chosen ⚑ Synthesized estimate Derived from training cost benchmarks comparing PINN vs standard NN on equivalent PDE benchmarks (Raissi et al., 2019; Karniadakis et al., 2021).
40% ML projects that fail to reach production ⚑ Survey-based estimate VentureBeat AI Failure Report (2021, n=2,196 enterprise ML projects); Gartner Hype Cycle for AI (2022).
O(n³) Worst-case matrix op complexity (naive dense inversion) Standard result for Gaussian elimination on dense n×n matrices (Golub & Van Loan, 4th ed., §3.1).
PINN Loss:   L = Ldata + λ · Lphysics ← physics constraint baked into the training objective
Plain language — what this formula means A PINN trains on two signals simultaneously. L_data: how well predictions match actual sensor measurements. L_physics: how much the predictions violate the known governing equation. The parameter λ controls the balance. Unlike a standard neural network, the PINN is penalised every time a prediction contradicts the physics.
Classical NN / Transformer

Data-Driven Approach

Learns patterns purely from data. Powerful generalisation but blind to physical laws. Requires large labeled datasets. Scales with data volume and compute.

Physics-Informed NN (PINN)

Physics + Data Hybrid

Embeds governing equations (PDEs/ODEs) via Lphysics in the loss function. Extrapolates physically. Works with sparse sensor data. Training is harder due to competing, stiff loss gradients (see Appendix A1).

04 — Scaling Intuition

When does more = better?

Scaling laws describe how training loss decreases as data, parameters, or compute increase. PINNs and NNs follow different scaling curves — they cross at a regime boundary — and PINNs plateau earlier in the high-data regime due to training stiffness from competing loss gradients.

● Literature data points anchored to published results (Raissi et al. 2019; Hoffmann et al. 2022 Chinchilla; Wang et al. 2021). Both axes log-scale (conceptual). PINN starts with lower loss in the low-data regime; NN overtakes as data scales.
Low-Data Regime — PINN Wins
Physics acts as a massive implicit regulariser — the model cannot overfit to noise because the PDE loss anchors every prediction. This is the primary engineering use case for PINNs: expensive physical experiments yield only sparse sensor readings, but the governing equations fill in the spatial gaps.
High-Data Regime — NN Wins
Classical NNs and Transformers scale better with data. PINNs plateau earlier — the physics loss creates gradient conflict (Wang et al., 2021, SISC 43(5)). Two competing objectives prevent the model from squeezing out the final error that a pure data-driven model achieves freely.
Chinchilla Scaling Law Analogy (Hoffmann et al., 2022)

For LLMs: optimal model size ∝ dataset size — scale both proportionally. For PINNs: optimal collocation point count ∝ PDE solution complexity. Do not blindly scale model parameters without proportionally scaling the training signal.

🚨 Data Leakage — What It Is & Why It's Catastrophic
  • Definition: When information from the test/validation set contaminates the training process. The model appears better than it is — it is effectively "cheating" on the exam.
  • Case 1 — Normalisation: Computing μ/σ across the entire dataset before splitting. Fix: fit scalers only on training fold, then transform test with the same scaler.
  • Case 2 — Temporal split: In time-series physical systems (e.g., heat flow), a random train/test split leaks future states into training. Fix: always split by time — first 80% of timesteps for training, last 20% for testing.
  • Case 3 — PINN boundary conditions: Using the same boundary condition measurements in both the training physics loss and the validation metric. Fix: maintain a held-out boundary test set used only for evaluation.
  • Consequence: Validation loss looks excellent. Real-world performance collapses. Particularly dangerous in safety-critical systems (aerospace, nuclear, medical devices).
✓ Best Practice

Always fit preprocessing (scalers, encoders) only on training data. Use temporal or physics-domain-aware splits. Version your data with DVC or equivalent — data provenance is as important as code versioning.

PINN-Specific Rule

Strictly separate collocation points (interior domain, used in L_physics) from boundary condition sensor measurements (used in L_data). These must never overlap with the validation set. A dedicated boundary holdout set is mandatory for honest evaluation.

💡 Memory Bandwidth is the Real Bottleneck

In both architectures, the practical constraint is often memory bandwidth, not raw FLOPs. Matrix multiplications are memory-bound at small batch sizes — the GPU spends more time waiting for data from DRAM than performing arithmetic. PINN's collocation loop creates many small sequential operations, leading to poor GPU occupancy. Always profile with PyTorch Profiler or Nsight Compute before optimising.

Why PINN training steps are 3–5× more expensive A standard neural network gradient step = one forward pass + one backward pass. A PINN must additionally compute second-order derivatives of the output (e.g. ∂²u/∂x²) to evaluate the PDE residual. This requires running backpropagation through the backpropagation graph itself — second-order auto-differentiation. The full cost derivation is in Appendix A3.

08b — Critical Discussion

When PINNs Fail — And What To Do About It

PINNs are a powerful tool, but they are not universally superior. Understanding where they break down — with quantitative impact estimates — is as important as understanding where they shine.

⚡ High-Frequency / Multi-Scale Solutions
Neural networks have a known "spectral bias" — they learn low-frequency components first. For turbulent flows, standard PINNs converge to a smooth but incorrect approximation.
Impact: high-freq components (k>10) converge 10× slower · persistent >15% L2 error
Fix: Fourier feature embeddings or multi-scale PINN architectures.
Ref: Rahaman et al. (2019, ICML) — spectral bias in deep networks
⚡ Stiff PDEs (sharp gradients)
When the PDE solution has sharp boundary layers (e.g., shock fronts), the loss landscape becomes extremely ill-conditioned.
Impact: >60% PINN failure rate for convection-dominated PDEs (Péclet >100) without RAR
Fix: Residual-based adaptive refinement (RAR).
Ref: Lu et al. (2021, SIAM Rev.) — DeepXDE adaptive sampling
⚡ Long Time-Horizon Problems
Standard PINNs on long temporal windows often suffer catastrophic error accumulation — the physics residual explodes as time increases.
Impact: for t > 5 char. time units, error grows 5–20× vs causal-trained PINN
Fix: Causal training (Wang et al., 2022) or domain decomposition ("XPINNs").
Ref: Wang et al. (2022, arXiv:2203.07404) — causal training
⚡ Inverse Problems with Noisy Data
Noise in boundary/sensor data is amplified by second-order derivatives in the physics loss.
Impact: 5% sensor noise → 25–40% error in inferred α (5–8× amplification)
Fix: Bayesian PINN variants; GP-based uncertainty quantification.
Ref: Yang et al. (2021, JCP) — Bayesian PINNs for noisy inverse problems
⚡ High-Dimensional PDEs (d > 4)
Collocation points required grow exponentially with dimension.
Impact: at d=6, n_c = O(10⁸) — ~10,000× more points than d=2
Fix: Deep BSDE methods, or quasi-Monte Carlo collocation.
Ref: Mishra & Molinaro (2023, IMA J. Numer. Anal. 43(1))
⚡ Gradient Pathology — L_physics Dominates
If λ is too large, the physics gradient completely overwhelms the data gradient.
Impact: gradient magnitude ratio up to 10⁶:1 without adaptive weighting
Fix: NTK-based adaptive λ scheduling recomputed each step.
Ref: Wang et al. (2021, SISC 43(5)) — NTK-based loss weighting
09 — Summary & Takeaways

The 3 Things engineering management needs to know

01

Architecture is a Data Decision

Don't pick NN vs PINN based on hype. Ask: how much labeled data is available, and do we need physics compliance? Classical NNs need data volume. PINNs need governing equations. For standard forward solves: FEM may simply be the right tool.

02

Matrix Ops = Cloud Bill

O(n²) attention and O(n_c × L × d²) PINN auto-diff translate directly to compute cost. PINN steps are 3–5× more expensive due to second-order differentiation. Always profile before optimising — memory bandwidth is usually the real constraint.

03

Data Leakage Kills Silently

Models with leakage look excellent in validation and fail in production. Temporal splits, correct normalisation scope, and separated collocation vs. boundary holdout data are non-negotiable in safety-critical physical systems.

The Honest Bottom Line

For physical systems with sparse data and hard physics constraints, PINNs deliver demonstrably better extrapolation and physics compliance — conservative estimates show 1.8% vs. 7.2% L2 error in the sparse-sensor regime and 15× lower physics residual than a classical NN. But PINNs are not a free lunch: they cost 3–5× more to train, fail on high-frequency and long-horizon problems, and require careful λ scheduling. FEM remains a better-validated choice for standard forward solves. The engineering decision is not "which is better?" — it is "which is better for this data regime, this compute budget, and this physics?"

Technical Appendix — For ML/Scientific Computing Readers

Deep Dive: PINN Training & Complexity

★ This appendix targets readers with ML or scientific computing background. The main deck (Slides 1–10) is fully self-contained without it.

A1 — PINN Training Stiffness & NTK-Based Loss Weighting (Wang et al., 2021, SISC 43(5))

The core training challenge is that L_data and L_physics produce gradients of vastly different magnitudes. The fix is adaptive loss weighting based on Neural Tangent Kernel (NTK) analysis:

λ_opt(t) = max_θ |∇_θ L_data| / mean_θ |∇_θ L_physics| ← recomputed each training step

Without NTK-based weighting, PINNs for the heat equation typically require 5–10× more epochs to reach equivalent accuracy.

A2 — Causal Training for Time-Dependent PDEs (Wang et al., 2022, arXiv:2203.07404)

Standard PINN training applies the physics loss uniformly across all time steps simultaneously. Causal weighting:

L_causal = Σ_i w_i · L_PDE(t_i), w_i = exp(−ε · Σ_{j<i} L_PDE(t_j))

The weight w_i is suppressed until earlier time steps have low residual. Reduces required training iterations by 5–10× for transient problems.

A3 — Auto-Differentiation Cost Derivation (Raissi et al., 2019, §3)

To evaluate ∂²u/∂x², automatic differentiation must traverse the computation graph twice. For a network with L layers of width d and n_c collocation points:

Cost_PINN ≈ n_c · (5 × C_fwd) + N_d · (2 × C_fwd) vs Cost_NN ≈ N · (2 × C_fwd)

The 5× factor = 1 forward + 2 backward (first derivative) + 2 backward (second derivative). For n_c = 10,000 and N_d = 200: PINN cost ≈ 50,400 equivalent forward passes vs 400 for a standard NN — a ~126× cost multiplier at this collocation density.

⚠ Reconciling 3–5× (Slide 6) vs ~126× (this appendix) — they measure different things:

3–5× per-step wall-clock ratio = empirical GPU timing comparison of one PINN training iteration vs one NN training iteration on the same hardware, at typical batch sizes used in practice (Raissi et al., 2019, reported experiments). GPU parallelism, kernel fusion, and memory prefetching partially amortise the theoretical cost — so wall-clock overhead is much smaller than the raw FLOP ratio.

~126× FLOP ratio = theoretical equivalent-forward-pass count derived from the formula above, assuming n_c=10k and N_data=200. This is the arithmetic cost ratio, not the wall-clock ratio. It grows linearly with n_c — at n_c=1,000 the ratio drops to ~13×; at n_c=100k it reaches ~1,260×.

Summary: 3–5× is what you observe on a profiler. ~126× is why it happens mathematically. Both numbers are correct — they answer different questions.

A4 — Flash Attention: IO Complexity Reduction (Dao et al., NeurIPS 2022)

Standard self-attention materialises the full n×n score matrix in GPU HBM, requiring O(n²·d) HBM reads/writes.

Standard: HBM I/O ∝ n²·d | Flash Attn: HBM I/O ∝ n·d·(M)^(1/2)

Flash Attention tiles computation so Q, K, V blocks fit entirely in SRAM. Output is bit-for-bit identical to standard attention. For n=8k tokens: memory reduced from ~8 GB to ~300 MB at FP16.

A5 — Collocation Scaling (Mishra & Molinaro, 2023, IMA J. Numer. Anal. 43(1))

n_c = O(ε^{-2(1+d/α)}) where α = Sobolev regularity of PDE solution, ε = target error

For the 2D heat equation (d=2, α≈2, target ε=10⁻³): n_c ≈ O(10⁶) worst case. Adaptive collocation via DeepXDE's PDEPointResampler reduces this to 10k–50k points in practice.

A6 — FEM vs PINN: Formal Accuracy Comparison

FEM (standard linear elements, uniform mesh) achieves O(h²) convergence in L2 norm for the heat equation. For a 100×100 grid (h=0.01): expected L2 error ≈ O(10⁻⁴). PINN achieves empirically 1–5% L2 error on the same problem without convergence guarantees. FEM's advantage is provable, mesh-dependent convergence. PINN's advantage is mesh-free operation and native inverse problem support.
