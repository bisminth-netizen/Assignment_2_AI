01 — The Engineering Problem

Why does model choice matter so much?

Choosing the wrong architecture for a physical system isn't just an academic mistake — it costs real compute time, real money, and produces models that hallucinate physics they were never taught.

5–10× Extra compute — wrong architecture choice ⚑ Synthesized estimate Raissi et al., 2019, J. Comput. Phys. 378, Table 2; Karniadakis et al., 2021, Nat. Rev. Phys. 3(6).
~40% ML projects failing to reach production ⚑ Survey estimate VentureBeat AI Failure Report (2021, n=2,196); Gartner Hype Cycle 2022, G00771258.
O(n³) Naive dense matrix inversion Golub & Van Loan, Matrix Computations 4th ed., §3.1.
PINN Loss:   L = Ldata + λ · Lphysics ← physics constraint baked into training
Plain language A PINN trains on two signals. L_data: how well predictions match sensor measurements. L_physics: how much predictions violate the governing equation. λ balances them. Unlike a standard NN, the PINN is penalised every time it contradicts the physics — making it impossible to "forget" the laws of the system.
Classical NN / Transformer

Data-Driven

Learns patterns purely from data. Powerful generalisation but blind to physical laws. Requires large labeled datasets.

Physics-Informed NN (PINN)

Physics + Data Hybrid

Embeds governing equations via Lphysics in the loss. Extrapolates physically. Works with sparse sensor data. Training harder due to competing, stiff gradients.

03 — Matrix Operation Bottlenecks

Where the compute dies

Every forward pass is a chain of matrix multiplications. Before we can understand why attention is O(n²) or why PINNs cost 3–5×, we need to see exactly how a matrix multiply works — and count the operations.

🔢 Dense Layer: O(B × d_in × d_out)
Each output cell = d_in multiplications + d_in additions.
Total cells = B × d_out. Hence cost = O(B · d_in · d_out).
Double d → cost ×4. Double B → cost ×2.
O(B·d_in·d_out) — predictable
⚡ Self-Attention: O(n² × d)
Attention score Sij = Qi·KjT — every token queries every other token.
n tokens → n² scores. Each score = d ops. Total = O(n²·d).
Double sequence length (512→1024) → cost ×4. That's the wall.
O(n²·d) — quadratic explosion
Plain language — the "dot product" intuition Imagine row i of matrix A = a list of 256 numbers. Column j of matrix B = another list of 256 numbers. Multiplying them = multiply each pair and add them all up = 256 multiplications + 256 additions. That's one output cell. For a 512×512 weight matrix: 512 × 512 output cells × 512 ops = 134 million operations per layer. Now you see why width (d) matters more than depth (L) for cost: cost scales as d², not d.

04 — Scaling Intuition

When does more = better?

Scaling laws describe how training loss decreases as data, parameters, or compute increase. PINNs and NNs follow different scaling curves — they cross at a regime boundary.

Low-Data Regime — PINN Wins
Physics acts as a massive implicit regulariser. Expensive physical experiments yield only sparse sensor readings, but the governing equations fill in spatial gaps — 10k+ collocation points replace expensive measurements.
High-Data Regime — NN Wins
Classical NNs scale better with data. PINNs plateau earlier — the physics loss creates gradient conflict (Wang et al., 2021, SISC 43(5)). Two competing objectives prevent squeezing out the final error a pure data-driven model achieves freely.
Chinchilla Analogy (Hoffmann et al., 2022)

For LLMs: optimal model size ∝ dataset size. For PINNs: optimal collocation count ∝ PDE solution complexity. The principle is identical: don't scale parameters without proportionally scaling the training signal.

Empirical basis Chart is conceptually derived from Wang et al. (2021, SISC) showing PINN stagnation due to gradient imbalance, and Mishra & Molinaro (2023, IMA J. Numer. Anal.) proving PINN error decays slower than NN as n → ∞. Exact crossover is problem-specific.

💡 Memory Bandwidth is the Real Bottleneck

Matrix multiplications are memory-bound at small batch sizes — the GPU spends more time waiting for data from DRAM than doing arithmetic. PINN's collocation loop creates many small sequential ops, leading to poor GPU occupancy. Always profile with PyTorch Profiler or Nsight Compute before optimising.

Why PINN steps cost 3–5× Standard NN = 1 forward + 1 backward pass. PINN must compute ∂²u/∂x² — second-order derivatives — by running backprop through the backprop graph itself. That's 2 extra passes per collocation point. Full derivation in Appendix A3.

08b — Critical Discussion

When PINNs Fail

A responsible engineering recommendation must address failure modes. PINNs are not universally superior — here are six cases where they break down and what to do about it.

⚡ High-Frequency / Multi-Scale
Spectral bias — NNs learn low-frequency components first. For turbulent flows, standard PINNs converge to a smooth but incorrect approximation. Fix: Fourier feature embeddings.
Rahaman et al., ICML 2019 — spectral bias
⚡ Stiff PDEs (Sharp Gradients)
Sharp boundary layers cause ill-conditioned loss landscapes. Training fails without adaptive collocation near high-gradient zones. Fix: Residual-based adaptive refinement (RAR).
Lu et al., SIAM Rev. 2021 — DeepXDE
⚡ Long Time Horizons
Error accumulates catastrophically over long windows. The model satisfies the PDE at early times but drifts arbitrarily at late times. Fix: Causal training (Wang et al., 2022).
Wang et al., arXiv:2203.07404
⚡ Noisy Inverse Problems
Sensor noise is amplified by second-order derivatives in the physics loss. Small error in u → large error in ∂²u/∂x². Fix: Bayesian PINN variants with GP-based uncertainty.
Yang et al., JCP 2021 — B-PINNs
⚡ High-Dimensional PDEs (d > 4)
Collocation points required grow exponentially with dimension — curse of dimensionality. Intractable at d=6+. Fix: Deep BSDE methods, quasi-Monte Carlo sampling.
Mishra & Molinaro, IMA J. Numer. Anal. 2023
⚡ Gradient Pathology (λ Imbalance)
Too large λ → physics overwhelms data. Too small → physics ignored. Manual λ tuning is brittle. Fix: NTK-based adaptive λ scheduling each step.
Wang et al., SISC 43(5) 2021
✅ When PINNs Work Best
✓ Sparse sensor data on well-understood PDE
✓ Smooth solutions, no sharp gradients
✓ Short-to-medium time horizons
✓ Safety-critical physics compliance required
✓ Low-dimensional domains (d ≤ 3)
✓ Low-noise inverse problems, known governing eq.
09 — Summary & Takeaways

The 3 Things engineering management needs to know

01

Architecture is a Data Decision

Don't pick NN vs PINN based on hype. Ask: how much labeled data is available, and do we need physics compliance? Classical NNs need data volume. PINNs need governing equations. The answer changes entirely with the data regime.

02

Matrix Ops = Cloud Bill

O(n²) attention and O(n_c×L×d²) PINN auto-diff translate directly to compute cost. PINN steps are 3–5× more expensive (Raissi 2019). Memory bandwidth — not FLOPs — is usually the real constraint.

03

Leakage Kills Silently

Models with leakage look excellent in validation and fail in production. Temporal splits, correct normalisation scope, and separated collocation vs. boundary holdout data are non-negotiable in safety-critical systems.

The Honest Bottom Line

For physical systems with sparse data and hard physics constraints, PINNs deliver demonstrably better extrapolation and compliance — mock benchmark shows 1.8% vs. 7.2% L2 error in the sparse-sensor regime and 15× lower physics residual. But PINNs cost 3–5× more to train, fail on high-frequency and long-horizon problems, and require careful λ scheduling. The engineering question is not "which is better?" — it is "which is better for this data regime, this compute budget, and this physics?" Start with PINN when data is scarce. Scale to NN surrogates when throughput matters. Profile everything first.

Sparse data → PINN Large data → NN Real-time → Distil to NN surrogate Always profile first

Technical Appendix — For ML/Scientific Computing Readers

Deep Dive: PINN Training & Complexity

★ Targets readers with ML or scientific computing background. Main deck (Slides 1–10) is fully self-contained without it.

A1 — PINN Training Stiffness & NTK-Based Loss Weighting (Wang et al., 2021)

L_data and L_physics produce gradients of vastly different magnitudes — "gradient pathology." The fix is NTK-based adaptive weighting:

λ_opt(t) = max_θ |∇_θ L_data| / mean_θ |∇_θ L_physics| ← recomputed each step

Without this, PINNs for the heat equation require 5–10× more epochs and often fail to converge for stiff PDEs entirely.

A2 — Causal Training (Wang et al., 2022)

Standard PINNs apply the physics loss uniformly across all time steps — causally incorrect for parabolic PDEs.

L_causal = Σ_i w_i · L_PDE(t_i), w_i = exp(−ε · Σ_{j<i} L_PDE(t_j))

Weight w_i is suppressed until earlier timesteps have low residual. Reduces iterations by 5–10× for transient problems.

A3 — Auto-Diff Cost Derivation (Raissi et al., 2019, §3)

Cost_PINN ≈ n_c · (5 × C_fwd) + N_d · (2 × C_fwd) vs Cost_NN ≈ N · (2 × C_fwd)

The 5× factor = 1 fwd + 2 bwd (1st deriv) + 2 bwd (2nd deriv). For n_c=10k, N_d=200: PINN ≈ 50,400 equivalent forward passes vs 400 for NN → ~126× cost multiplier.

A4 — Flash Attention IO Analysis (Dao et al., 2022)

Standard: HBM I/O ∝ n²·d | Flash Attn: HBM I/O ∝ n·d·√M

Flash Attention tiles Q,K,V into SRAM. Full attention matrix never written to HBM. Identical output — IO-complexity result, not approximation. n=8k: ~8 GB → ~300 MB at FP16.

A5 — Collocation Scaling (Mishra & Molinaro, 2023)

n_c = O(ε^{-2(1+d/α)}) α = Sobolev regularity, ε = target error

For 2D heat equation (d=2, α≈2, ε=10⁻³): n_c ≈ O(10⁶) worst case. Adaptive collocation (DeepXDE PDEPointResampler) reduces to 10k–50k in practice.
